▲ CPI-based Real Rate — Market Standard
Traders, analysts, and most finance textbooks use Real Rate = Nominal Rate − CPI YoY. CPI is widely published and easy to access. More importantly, TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities) spreads are calculated using CPI — so bond markets are priced off CPI, not PCE. When the CPI-based real rate turns positive, market conditions are officially restrictive: borrowing is expensive in real terms and growth slows.
▲ PCE-based Real Rate — Fed Preferred
The Federal Reserve officially targets 2% inflation using PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures) — not CPI. PCE covers a broader basket of goods and adjusts for substitution effects (when consumers switch to cheaper alternatives). PCE typically runs 0.3–0.5% below CPI. In FOMC meetings, press conferences, and the dot plot, all inflation references are PCE-based. This is the number Powell watches most closely when deciding rate policy.

▼ Inversion (Spread < 0)
When 2Y yield exceeds 10Y yield, short-term borrowing costs more than long-term. This signals markets expect the Fed to cut rates — typically following a recession. Every U.S. recession since 1978 was preceded by a yield curve inversion.
▲ Normal Curve (Spread > 0)
A positively sloped yield curve means longer-term bonds yield more than short-term — healthy sign of economic growth expectations. The curve is re-steepening now as the Fed cuts short-end rates while long-end stays elevated on inflation fears.
⚠ Bull vs Bear Steepening
Bull Steepening: Short-end falls (Fed cuts) — bullish for equities. Bear Steepening: Long-end rises (inflation fears) — bearish for bonds. Current re-steepening is a mix — 2Y falling on cuts, 10Y sticky on tariff/fiscal concerns.

How the Taylor Rule Works
The Taylor Rule (1993) provides a formula for central banks to set the optimal interest rate: i = r* + π + 0.5(π − π*) + 0.5(y) where r* is the neutral real rate, π is current inflation, π* is the target (2%), and y is the output gap. A positive gap means the implied rate is above actual — suggesting the Fed is too accommodative. Critics argue the rule is mechanical and ignores financial stability, global factors, and uncertainty.

Why Prices Fall When Rates Rise
A bond pays fixed coupons. If new bonds are issued at higher rates, your old lower-coupon bond becomes less valuable — its price drops until its yield matches the new market rate. This is the fundamental law of fixed income.
Duration — The Sensitivity Measure
Duration measures how sensitive a bond's price is to rate changes. A 10-year duration means a 1% rate rise = ~10% price fall. Longer maturity = higher duration = more risk. During the 2022 hike cycle, 20Y+ bond funds lost 30–40%.
Trading Application
Fed cutting → buy long-duration bonds (TLT). Fed hiking → short bonds / stay short-duration. In 2024, bond traders who anticipated cuts early made 15–25% on TLT as the pivot trade. Timing the turn is the key skill.

How the FedWatch Tool Works
CME FedWatch analyzes the prices of 30-Day Fed Funds Futures contracts — financial instruments that traders use to bet on where the Fed rate will be each month. Because real money is at stake, futures prices reflect the market's collective best estimate of Fed decisions. The tool converts these prices into probabilities for each FOMC meeting outcome: Hold, Cut −25bps, Cut −50bps, or Hike. When a probability exceeds 80%, the market consensus is strong. Markets are "forward-looking" — they price in what they expect the Fed to do, not just what it has done.

What Is a TIPS Breakeven Rate?
TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities) are government bonds whose principal adjusts with CPI. The breakeven rate = Nominal Treasury Yield − TIPS Real Yield. It represents the average annual CPI inflation rate at which buying TIPS vs. nominal bonds gives the same return. If actual inflation exceeds the breakeven, TIPS outperform. If below, nominal bonds win. It is the purest market-based gauge of inflation expectations.
Why It Matters for Traders
Rising breakevens → market fears higher inflation → bond yields rise, gold rallies, DXY weakens. Falling breakevens → deflation fears → bonds rally, real yields rise, gold falls. The 5Y5Y forward breakeven (inflation expected 5–10 years out) is what the Fed watches most closely. Currently 5Y breakevens (2.56%) are elevated, suggesting tariff-driven inflation fears are real but not yet entrenched in long-run expectations.

QE — Quantitative Easing
The Fed buys Treasuries and MBS, injecting cash into the banking system (bank reserves increase). This pushes long-term yields down, boosts asset prices, and stimulates lending. Used during GFC (2008–2014) and COVID (2020–2022). The balance sheet grew from $900B (2007) to $8.9T (2022) — nearly 10×.
QT — Quantitative Tightening
The Fed allows maturing bonds to "roll off" without reinvestment — draining reserves from the system. At peak QT (2022–2024), Treasuries rolled off at $60B/month + MBS at $35B/month. This is the opposite of QE — it reduces liquidity and puts upward pressure on long-term yields. QT ended December 1, 2025 when reserves reached "ample" levels (~$3.2T).
Market Impact
A larger Fed balance sheet suppresses long-term yields by 2–4% (BlackRock 2024 estimate). QE was like "shadow rate cuts" beyond the 0% floor. Conversely, QT is a shadow tightening on top of rate hikes. This is why the 2022–23 tightening was the most aggressive in 40 years — both tools worked simultaneously. QT ending in Dec 2025 removes a hidden headwind to markets.

Inflation Measurement
CPI, PCE & How Inflation Is Measured
Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices rises over time. Two separate government agencies measure it differently — and understanding the difference is critical for reading Fed policy correctly.
CPI
Consumer Price
Index
Published by BLS
Monthly
Market Standard
The Consumer Price Index measures the average change in prices paid by urban consumers for a fixed basket of goods and services over time.
The CPI is published by the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) around the 10th–15th of each month, covering the prior month. It tracks a fixed "basket" of ~80,000 goods and services across 8 major categories: Food, Energy, Shelter, Medical Care, Transportation, Apparel, Recreation, and Education.
How it is calculated: BLS surveys ~23,000 retail outlets and 50,000 housing units across 87 urban areas each month. The basket weights are fixed (updated every 2 years from consumer expenditure surveys) — meaning if beef prices spike and consumers switch to chicken, CPI still tracks beef at full original weight. This is called the substitution bias — a known limitation.
Formula
CPI = (Cost of Basket in Current Period ÷ Cost of Basket in Base Period) × 100

Inflation Rate (YoY) = (CPIcurrent − CPI1 year ago) ÷ CPI1 year ago × 100%
Headline CPI includes all items. Core CPI excludes food and energy (too volatile) to show underlying trend. Current: Headline 3.0% YoY (Jan 2026), Core 3.3% YoY.
Why Traders Watch CPI
CPI is the most market-moving inflation release. Published before PCE, so it often sets the tone. Higher-than-expected CPI → bond sell-off, USD rally, equity dip. TIPS spreads and FedWatch probabilities move immediately after CPI day.
Why the Fed Does NOT Target CPI
CPI has a known upward bias (~0.3–0.5% above PCE) due to its fixed basket. The Fed prefers PCE because it better reflects actual consumer behavior. However, the Fed still monitors CPI because it affects wage negotiations, Social Security adjustments, and market expectations.

PCE
Personal Consumption
Expenditures Deflator
Published by BEA
Monthly
Fed's Official Target
The PCE Price Index measures changes in the prices of goods and services consumed by households in the United States, using a chain-weighted methodology that adjusts for consumer substitution.
The PCE is published by the Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA), typically in the last week of each month. It draws data from business surveys (not just consumer surveys like CPI) and uses a chain-weighted basket — meaning if consumers substitute beef for chicken, PCE automatically adjusts its basket weights. This removes the substitution bias present in CPI.
Why the Fed chose PCE: In 2000, the FOMC switched its official inflation benchmark from CPI to PCE. Reasons: (1) PCE covers a broader set of expenditures including employer-paid healthcare; (2) chain-weighting is more accurate; (3) PCE has smaller historical revisions; (4) PCE better captures the actual cost of living. All FOMC projections, dot plots, and Powell's press conference references use PCE.
Fed's Policy Framework (adopted Aug 2020)
Fed Target: Core PCE = 2.0% (average over time, flexible)
Current Core PCE: 2.8% (Dec 2025) → 0.8% above target
Current Headline PCE: 2.9% (Dec 2025) → 0.9% above target
Core PCE excludes food and energy. This is the Fed's single most-watched number. Super-Core PCE (services ex-housing) is an even newer metric that Powell has cited since 2023 — it strips out both goods and housing to isolate sticky services inflation driven by wages.
Trading PCE Releases
PCE comes out ~3 weeks after CPI. By then, market often has a rough estimate. But surprises still move markets: hot PCE → fewer cuts priced in → bonds sell, USD rallies. PCE day is less volatile than CPI day because it is the "second read" on inflation.

Bonds, Yields, TIPS & the Breakeven Rate
Bonds are loans to governments or companies. Understanding how their prices and yields move is foundational to all macro trading — because the bond market is the largest and most liquid market in the world ($140 trillion globally).
Bond

Government / Corporate

Maturity Date
A bond is a loan from an investor to a borrower (government or corporation) in which the borrower promises to pay regular interest (coupon) and return the principal (face value) at a specified maturity date.
When the US government needs money, it does not print it — it borrows by issuing Treasury bonds. An investor buys the bond, lending money to the government. In return, the government pays a fixed coupon (interest payment) every 6 months and returns the full face value ($1,000 or $100) at the end of the term (maturity).
Bond Anatomy — Example: 10-Year Treasury at 4.5% Coupon
Face Value: $1,000
Coupon Rate: 4.5% per year → $45/year → $22.50 every 6 months
Maturity: 10 years
At maturity: investor receives final $22.50 coupon + $1,000 principal back
Types of US Treasury bonds by maturity: T-Bills (1 month–1 year) · T-Notes (2–10 years) · T-Bonds (20–30 years). The 10-Year Treasury Note is the global benchmark — it is the most important interest rate in the world because it prices mortgages, corporate loans, and acts as the "risk-free rate" for all financial models.
Critical inverse relationship: When interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall — and vice versa. Why? If you hold a bond paying 3% and new bonds now pay 5%, your bond is less attractive. Its price must fall until its effective yield equals 5%. This is the core mechanic behind all bond trading.
Trading Bonds
TLT = 20+ Year Treasury ETF. When you expect rate cuts → buy TLT (bond prices rise). When you expect hikes or hot inflation → short TLT. Duration matters: a 30Y bond moves much more in price for each 1% rate change than a 2Y bond.
Bond Risks
Interest Rate Risk — price falls when rates rise.
Inflation Risk — fixed coupon loses real value.
Credit Risk — borrower may default (not relevant for US Treasuries).
Liquidity Risk — hard to sell in a panic.

Yield to Maturity
(YTM)

Inverse to Price
Risk-Free Rate
The bond yield (Yield to Maturity) is the total annualized return an investor earns if they buy the bond at today's market price and hold it until maturity, receiving all coupons and the face value at the end.
While the coupon rate is fixed at issuance, the yield changes every second in the market as bond prices fluctuate. If you buy a $1,000 bond at a discount of $950, your effective yield is higher than the stated coupon. If you pay a premium of $1,050, your yield is lower.
Simplified Yield Formula
Current Yield = Annual Coupon Payment ÷ Current Market Price × 100

Example: Bond paying $45/yr, currently priced at $950:
Current Yield = $45 ÷ $950 = 4.74% (higher than 4.5% coupon rate)
Why yield matters more than price: Yield is the language of bond markets. When traders say "the 10Y is at 4.3%," they mean the 10-Year Treasury yield — which directly affects mortgage rates, corporate borrowing costs, stock valuations (via the discount rate), and currency markets. Every basis point (0.01%) move in the 10Y ripples through the entire financial system.
Yield curve: Plotting yields across maturities (2Y, 5Y, 10Y, 30Y) creates the yield curve. Normally it slopes upward (longer maturity = higher yield, compensating for more risk and inflation over time). When it inverts (short-term yields exceed long-term), it historically signals a recession is coming.

Why 10Y Rules All Markets
P/E ratios on stocks are calculated using 10Y as risk-free rate. Mortgage rates = 10Y + ~1.5%. Corporate bond rates = 10Y + credit spread. If 10Y rises → stocks become expensive (their "competition" from safe bonds improves) → equity valuations compress.

Inflation Hedge
TIPS are US government bonds whose principal value adjusts with CPI inflation. The coupon rate is fixed, but since it is applied to an inflation-adjusted principal, the actual coupon payment rises with inflation — protecting investors from purchasing power loss.
TIPS were introduced by the US Treasury in 1997. Unlike regular Treasuries, a TIPS bond's face value moves with CPI. If CPI rises 3% in a year, the TIPS principal grows by 3%, and the next coupon is calculated on the larger principal. At maturity, you receive either the inflation-adjusted principal or the original face value — whichever is greater.
TIPS Mechanics — Example
Purchase: $1,000 face value, 1% coupon
Year 1 CPI: +3%
Adjusted Principal: $1,000 × 1.03 = $1,030
Year 1 Coupon Payment: 1% × $1,030 = $10.30 (not just $10.00)
→ Your real return is preserved regardless of inflation.
TIPS Real Yield — the yield on a TIPS bond is a real yield, already inflation-adjusted. When TIPS real yields are positive, investors are earning above inflation. When negative (as during 2020–2022), you are effectively paying to be protected — the market is so worried about inflation that negative real yields are accepted. In early 2022, 10Y TIPS yielded −1.0%+ — an extreme signal.
When to Buy TIPS
Buy TIPS when you expect actual inflation to exceed breakeven rate. If the 10Y breakeven is 2.3% and you believe CPI will average 3.5%, TIPS will outperform regular Treasuries. Conversely, if you believe disinflation is coming, regular bonds win.
TIPS as Fed Signal
The Fed watches TIPS-derived breakeven rates as a real-time gauge of inflation expectations. If 5Y breakeven spikes above 2.5–3%, the Fed interprets this as expectations becoming "unanchored" — justifying more aggressive action. Currently 5Y breakeven = 2.56% (elevated vs 2% target).

The breakeven inflation rate is the average annual CPI inflation rate at which a TIPS bond and a regular nominal Treasury bond of the same maturity produce identical total returns. It represents the bond market's consensus expectation for future inflation.
The Core Formula
Breakeven Rate = Nominal Treasury Yield − TIPS Real Yield

Example (10Y, Mar 2026):
10Y Treasury Yield: 4.72%
10Y TIPS Real Yield: 2.41%
10Y Breakeven: 4.72% − 2.41% = 2.31%
→ Market expects CPI to average 2.31% over the next 10 years
If actual inflation over 10 years turns out to be above 2.31%, TIPS investors win. If below 2.31%, nominal Treasury investors win. The breakeven is the "fair price" at which both are equal — hence the name.
5Y5Y Forward Breakeven — this measures inflation expected 5 years from now over the following 5 years (years 5–10). It strips out near-term noise and shows truly long-run expectations. This is the metric the Fed watches most closely. If 5Y5Y rises above 2.5%, it signals markets no longer believe inflation will return to 2% — a policy emergency.

The real interest rate is the nominal interest rate adjusted for inflation. It represents the true purchasing-power cost of borrowing money — or the true return on saving after accounting for inflation erosion.
Fisher Equation (simplified)
Real Rate ≈ Nominal Rate − Inflation Rate

Version 1 — Market Standard (CPI-based):
Real Rate = Fed Funds Rate − CPI YoY
= 3.65% − 3.0% = +0.65% (Mar 2026) → Restrictive

Version 2 — Fed Preferred (PCE-based):
Real Rate = Fed Funds Rate − PCE YoY
= 3.65% − 2.9% = +0.75% (Mar 2026) → Restrictive

Cycle Peak Real Rate (CPI): 5.33% − 3.0% = +2.33% (mid-2024) → Highly Restrictive
Positive real rate = Restrictive policy. When the real rate is positive, borrowing costs exceed inflation — money is genuinely expensive. This slows investment, credit growth, and economic activity. Negative real rate = Accommodative policy. In 2021–22, the Fed rate was near 0% while inflation hit 7–9%, creating a deeply negative real rate of −5% to −7% — extremely stimulative, and a primary cause of the inflation surge.
Trading the Real Rate
Rising real rates → USD strengthens, gold weakens, growth stocks sell off. Falling real rates → gold rallies, USD weakens, risk assets re-rate higher. The DXY has a very strong correlation with real rates — track both together.
The Neutral Real Rate (r*)
The Fed estimates a "neutral" real rate — the level that neither stimulates nor restricts. Currently estimated at ~0.5–1.0% (real). With current real rate at +0.65%, policy is marginally restrictive. The Taylor Rule uses this r* to derive an optimal Fed Funds target.

Federal Reserve Policy Framework
FOMC, Dot Plot, Yield Curve, QE & QT
The Federal Reserve controls monetary policy through a committee structure, forward guidance via its dot plot, and direct market operations through its balance sheet. Understanding how these tools work is essential to anticipating market moves.

The Federal Open Market Committee is the monetary policy-setting body of the Federal Reserve System. It meets 8 times per year to decide the target range for the federal funds rate and conduct open market operations.
The FOMC has 12 voting members: 7 members of the Federal Reserve Board of Governors (including the Chair, Jerome Powell) + 5 of the 12 Federal Reserve Bank presidents (New York always votes; the other 11 rotate). All 12 presidents attend and participate in discussions but only 5 vote at any given time.
The Dual Mandate: The Fed has two legally mandated goals — (1) Maximum Employment and (2) Price Stability (defined as 2% PCE inflation). When these two goals conflict (e.g., inflation is high but unemployment is also rising), the FOMC must make difficult trade-off decisions. This is why 2022–2023 was so historically consequential — they chose to fight inflation aggressively even at the risk of recession.
The Meeting Structure: Every FOMC meeting lasts 2 days. On Day 2, they vote and release a statement at 2:00 PM ET. The Chair holds a press conference at 2:30 PM. Four times per year (Mar, Jun, Sep, Dec), they also release the Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) which includes the famous Dot Plot.
FOMC Day Trading Guide
2:00 PM ET: Statement released → immediate bond + equity reaction. 2:30 PM: Powell press conference → often reverses or amplifies the move. Watch: tone on inflation ("still elevated" vs "making progress"), forward guidance ("data dependent"), and any surprise shifts from the dot plot.

The Dot Plot is a scatter chart released by the FOMC four times per year, showing each of the 19 committee members' individual projections for where the federal funds rate should be at year-end for the current year, the next 2–3 years, and "longer run."
Each of the 19 FOMC participants (7 Governors + 12 Reserve Bank Presidents) submits their anonymous forecast for the Fed Funds rate, GDP growth, unemployment, and PCE inflation. These are compiled into the Summary of Economic Projections (SEP). The rate forecasts are plotted as dots on a scatter chart — hence "dot plot."
What markets look for: The median dot — the middle dot in each year — represents the committee's central tendency and is the closest thing to an "official" forecast. If the median moves from 2 cuts to 1 cut, bonds sell off. If it moves from 1 cut to 3, bonds rally hard. The "longer run" dot — currently 2.875% — is the FOMC's estimate of the neutral rate (r*).

Trading the Dot Plot
Dot plot shifts are massive market events. A "hawkish shift" (fewer dots in the cut zone) → bonds sell, USD rallies, growth stocks drop. A "dovish shift" → bonds and equities rally, gold rises. Markets sometimes start pricing in the new dot plot direction weeks ahead based on Fed speeches.
Dot Plot Limitations
The dot plot is NOT a commitment. Dots can change dramatically between meetings based on data. In Sep 2023, dots implied no cuts until 2025 — yet the first cut came Sep 2024. Dots are "a useful guide, not a GPS." Always combine with FedWatch probabilities and CPI/PCE data for context.

The yield curve is a graph showing the yields of US Treasury bonds across all maturities at a single point in time. Its shape — normal, flat, or inverted — is one of the most powerful leading indicators of economic conditions.
Normal Yield Curve (upward sloping): Long-term rates > short-term rates. This is the natural state — investors demand a higher yield for tying up money longer and accepting more uncertainty. Indicates healthy economic expectations.
Inverted Yield Curve (downward sloping): Short-term rates > long-term rates. This happens when the Fed has hiked rates aggressively (pushing up the 2Y) while markets expect future rate cuts and slower growth (pulling down the 10Y). The 10Y−2Y spread has inverted before every US recession since 1955. The 2023–2024 inversion was the deepest in 40 years, reaching −1.07% in July 2023.

Trading the Yield Curve
Curve steepening → banks become more profitable (they borrow short, lend long) → KBE/KRE bank ETFs rally. Curve flattening/inversion → bank stocks sell off, defensive sectors outperform. Watching the curve helps anticipate sector rotation before earnings confirm it.

Quantitative Easing (QE) is the Fed's purchase of bonds to inject money into the financial system. Quantitative Tightening (QT) is the opposite — allowing bonds to mature without reinvestment, draining money from the system. Both are used when the Fed Funds Rate alone is insufficient.
Why QE/QT exists: The Fed's primary tool — the Fed Funds Rate — controls only overnight bank lending rates. But during crises (2008, 2020) rates hit 0% and couldn't go lower. QE was the "unconventional" tool that pushed long-term yields down even further by directly buying Treasuries and Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS). It is like a rate cut beyond what is mathematically possible with the short-term rate alone.
Fed Balance Sheet Timeline
2007 (pre-GFC): $900B → 2014 (QE1+2+3): $4.5T (QE I, II, III after 2008 crisis)
2020 (COVID QE): $4.2T → Apr 2022: $8.97T (+$4.7T in just 2 years)
Jun 2022: QT begins at $47.5B/month (Treasuries + MBS roll-off)
Sep 2022: QT pace doubled to $95B/month
Jun 2024: QT slowed to $60B/month (Treasuries only: $25B, MBS: $35B)
Dec 1, 2025: QT ends — reserves at "ample" level (~$3.2T)
Mar 2026 Balance Sheet: $6.60T
Market Impact of QT Ending
QT ending in Dec 2025 removes a significant liquidity headwind. The Fed now reinvests all maturing bonds, keeping the balance sheet stable. This is a subtle but real tailwind for risk assets in 2026 — liquidity is no longer being actively drained. Watch bank reserve levels for early warning of any renewed stress.
Shadow Rate Concept
Economists calculate a "shadow Fed Funds Rate" that combines the actual rate with QE/QT effects. At peak QE in 2021, the shadow rate was deeply negative (−3% to −4%) even though the official rate was 0%. In 2022–23, the combined shadow rate (rate hikes + QT) was far more restrictive than the 5.33% official rate alone.

Optimal Rate Formula
The Taylor Rule is a mathematical formula that calculates where the Fed Funds Rate "should" be, based on the current inflation deviation from target and the output gap (how far GDP is from potential). It is a policy benchmarking tool, not a binding rule.
Taylor Rule Formula (1993 Original)
i = r* + π + 0.5(π − π*) + 0.5(y)

Where:
i = Recommended Federal Funds Rate
r* = Neutral Real Rate (estimated ~0.5% currently by Fed)
π = Current inflation (PCE) = 2.9%
π* = Inflation target = 2.0%
y = Output gap (% by which GDP exceeds/lags potential) ≈ 0.5%

Calculation: i = 0.5 + 2.9 + 0.5(2.9−2.0) + 0.5(0.5)
i = 0.5 + 2.9 + 0.45 + 0.25 = 4.10%
Actual Rate: 3.65% → Taylor Rule says Fed is 45bps too loose
The Taylor Rule was created by Stanford economist John B. Taylor in 1993. It describes Fed policy during the "Great Moderation" (1987–2003) with surprising accuracy. When the Fed deviates significantly from the Taylor Rule — particularly by holding rates too low for too long (as in 2003–2004 and 2020–2022) — it often leads to asset bubbles or inflation.
Taylor Rule vs Actual Rate History
2021: Taylor Rule implied 4–6% (to fight inflation). Actual: 0–0.25%. Gap = −4 to −6%. This enormous gap fueled the inflation surge.
2023 Peak: Taylor implied ~4.5%. Actual: 5.33%. Fed slightly over-tightened.
Mar 2026: Taylor implies ~4.10%. Actual: 3.65%. Fed slightly loose.
Taylor Rule Limitations
The rule is highly sensitive to r* (neutral rate) — which is unobservable and debated. Different r* assumptions give very different results. Also doesn't account for financial stability concerns, global spillovers, or the zero lower bound. It is a useful benchmark, not a decision algorithm.

The CME FedWatch Tool extracts market-implied probabilities for each possible FOMC outcome (hold, cut, hike) from the prices of 30-Day Federal Funds Futures contracts traded on the CME exchange.
30-Day Fed Funds Futures are contracts whose settlement price is tied to the average effective Fed Funds Rate during the contract month. Because real money is at stake, their prices directly reflect traders' collective expectation of where rates will be. CME reverse-engineers the probabilities using a simple formula.
How Probabilities Are Extracted
Futures Price: 96.35 → Implied Rate: 100 − 96.35 = 3.65%

If current rate = 3.65% (hold) and cut target = 3.40% (−25bps):
Probability of Cut = (Implied Rate − Hold Rate) ÷ (Cut Rate − Hold Rate)
= (3.65 − 3.65) ÷ (3.40 − 3.65) = 0% (fully priced as hold)

If futures implied rate = 3.57%:
P(cut) = (3.65 − 3.57) ÷ (3.65 − 3.40) = 0.08 ÷ 0.25 = 32% probability
What "priced in" means: When the probability of an outcome exceeds ~80–90%, the market has already adjusted prices as if that outcome is certain. This means if the Fed does exactly what is expected, markets barely move. The biggest trading opportunities arise when the Fed surprises relative to what FedWatch priced in. Tracking FedWatch over 4–8 weeks before a meeting reveals whether consensus is building or shifting.
Using FedWatch in Trading
Monitor the probability shift trend, not just the snapshot. If P(hold) falls from 90% to 60% in 2 weeks → sell bonds, buy USD before the meeting. Trade the shift in expectations, not the meeting itself. By meeting day, the outcome is usually priced in — but Powell's tone creates fresh surprises.

The Core Principle — How Monetary Policy Moves the Economy
When the Federal Reserve changes the overnight interest rate, it triggers a delayed chain reaction across every corner of the economy. The effects are not instantaneous — they work through credit channels, consumer behavior, and business investment decisions with time lags of 6 to 24 months. This is why central banking is more art than science: by the time the effects of a hike are fully felt, the economy may have already shifted dramatically. Understanding these cause-and-effect relationships is foundational to reading any macro chart, trading any asset class, or forecasting any economic outcome.

Inflation Lag: 6–18 Months
Fed hiked 525bps from Mar 2022–Jul 2023. CPI peaked at 9.1% in Jun 2022 and only fell to ~3% by early 2024 — a full 18 months of lag. This is why the Fed cannot wait to see inflation fall before acting — they must act pre-emptively on models and expectations.
Unemployment Lag: 9–18 Months
After the first hike in Mar 2022, unemployment stayed near 50-year lows (3.4%) until mid-2024 — over 2 years later. Labor markets are the last to feel monetary tightening because companies are slow to lay off workers and prefer to reduce hours and hiring first.
GDP Lag: Complex & Non-Linear
GDP can diverge dramatically from expectations. Q3 2023 GDP surged to 4.9% even as rates peaked at 5.33% — defying every model. Why? $2T+ in fiscal stimulus, IRA/CHIPS Act spending, and AI-driven capex absorbed the rate shock. The drag finally arrived in Q4 2025 at 1.4%.

How Rate Hikes Reduce Inflation — 3 Channels
1. Demand Channel: Higher rates make borrowing expensive → consumers buy less → businesses cannot raise prices → demand-pull inflation falls.
2. Dollar Channel: Higher rates attract foreign capital → USD strengthens → imported goods become cheaper → import inflation falls.
3. Expectations Channel: When the Fed hikes credibly, households and businesses expect lower future inflation and stop raising prices pre-emptively — this alone reduces inflation.
The Last Mile Problem — Why 3%→2% Is So Hard
Shelter inflation (32% of CPI basket) lags by 12–18 months because apartment leases renew slowly. Even as home prices fell in 2023, CPI shelter kept rising into 2024. Services inflation (medical, education, haircuts) is driven by wages — sticky and hard to compress without mass unemployment. Tariff risk (2026): New trade tariffs add fresh cost-push inflation even as the Fed cuts — a uniquely difficult policy environment.

The Phillips Curve — Inflation vs Unemployment Trade-off
The Phillips Curve is the foundation of Fed policy. It states that there is an inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation — when unemployment is low, workers demand higher wages, businesses pass costs to prices, and inflation rises. When unemployment rises, wage pressure falls and inflation cools. The Fed exploits this: by hiking rates to create some unemployment, it deliberately slows inflation. The 2022–2025 cycle proved the curve still works: unemployment rose from 3.4% to 4.3% as CPI fell from 9.1% to ~3%.
The Soft Landing — Why This Cycle Was Exceptional
History shows that aggressive Fed tightening nearly always causes recession and mass unemployment (1981: 10.8%, 1991: 7.8%, 2009: 10%). The 2022–2025 cycle broke this pattern — unemployment only rose from 3.4% to 4.3%, and no recession occurred. Why? Strong household balance sheets from pandemic savings, $5T+ in cumulative fiscal stimulus, re-shoring demand (CHIPS/IRA acts), and a structural labor shortage in services sectors absorbed the rate shock. This is the definition of a "soft landing."

How High Rates Hurt GDP — All 4 Components
GDP = C + I + G + NX. High rates hit all four: C (Consumption) falls as credit cards, mortgages, and auto loans become expensive. I (Investment) collapses as business projects become unprofitable at high discount rates. G (Government) gets crowded out as interest on national debt consumes a larger budget share. NX (Net Exports) deteriorates as a stronger dollar makes US goods expensive abroad. The GDP drag typically peaks 12–24 months after the first hike.
The Q3 2023 Paradox — GDP Surged at Peak Rates
Classical theory predicted recession at 5.33% rates. Instead, Q3 2023 GDP printed 4.9% — highest since 2021. Three forces drove this anomaly: (1) Fiscal stimulus: IRA ($740B) and CHIPS Act ($52B) injected massive government spending into the economy. (2) Excess savings: Americans still had $1.5T in pandemic-era savings buffer. (3) AI Capex boom: Microsoft, Amazon, Google, and Meta spent over $200B on data centers in 2023–24, generating a private investment surge that offset rate pressure.

Why This Cycle Was Unique
The 2022–2025 tightening defied every textbook model. The US absorbed 525bps of hikes without recession, kept unemployment below 4.4%, and grew GDP at 2.8% in 2024. The lesson: fiscal policy can temporarily offset monetary tightening — but only temporarily. The fiscal tailwinds are now fading and the 2026 slowdown is the delayed echo of 2022–23 tightening.
Key Risks to Watch in 2026
Tariff pass-through: New trade tariffs could push CPI back toward 3.5–4% by Q3 2026. Labor market: Federal layoffs + slowing private hiring. If unemployment crosses 5%, recession probability rises sharply. Q1 2026 GDP: BEA advance estimate (Apr 2026) will confirm if deceleration is temporary — or the beginning of a contraction.
